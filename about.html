Talavou Malietoa Fitisemanu III

Talavou carries the heritage of Sapapali‘i in Samoa and Neiafu in Tonga, rooted in the proud lineages of the Fitisemanu, Tu‘imauga, Mann, and Wolfgramm families. Born in Los Angeles before moving to Samoa, his early life was shaped by both island traditions and the diverse landscapes of the western United States, where he grew up in Arizona, Northern California, and Utah.

Athletics first defined his path — playing American football and rugby at Snow College and the University of Utah — but his journey ultimately led him to the arts, where he completed his Bachelor of Arts at Brigham Young University. This transition from the field to the studio reflects a discipline forged in sport and a vision rooted in culture.

Fitisemanu’s art draws deeply on ancestral symbols, patterns, and motifs, reinterpreting them through a modern lens. His work honors sacrifice, respect, and the responsibility of carrying forward his royal lineage, while also engaging with global traditions to highlight shared values and collective experiences. His piece “Patterns of the Pacific” marked his museum debut at the Springville Museum of Art’s 100th Annual Spring Salon Show, a testament to his ability to bridge heritage and contemporary practice.

Guided by the belief that he is a “village-made” artist, Fitisemanu sees art as a communal force — a way to unite, uplift, and remind audiences of the enduring principles that bind us together. Looking ahead, he aims to travel the world to build bridges through art, honoring the histories of Indigenous peoples and strengthening bonds across cultures worldwide.
